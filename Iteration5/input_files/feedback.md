The current analysis provides a sophisticated thermodynamic screening, but it suffers from a significant methodological disconnect between the "mechanical integrity" filter and the final candidate selection.

**1. Critical Methodological Flaw: The "Volume Expansion" Paradox**
You identified 14 candidates with $|\Delta V| \le 20\%$ as the only mechanically robust materials. However, your final ranked list (Section 6) promotes materials like NaTiPO₅ ($\Delta V \approx 65\%$) and LiVPO₅ ($\Delta V \approx 30-40\%$). You explicitly state that massive expansion leads to "pore blockage and particle pulverization," yet you abandon your own strict filter to favor these materials. This is an overclaim: if the 20% threshold is physically meaningful, the top-ranked candidates are likely to fail in practice. You must either justify why these specific polyanionic frameworks can accommodate 40-60% volume expansion without failure (e.g., via specific lattice anisotropy or "breathing" mechanisms) or re-rank based strictly on the 20% threshold.

**2. Over-reliance on Empirical Proxies**
The use of $S \approx 3k_B$ per atom for solid-state entropy is a crude approximation that ignores the vibrational density of states (VDOS) differences between oxides and carbonates. Given that you have the full crystal structures, you should use the Debye model or the Materials Project's own vibrational entropy estimates if available. Furthermore, the Tamman temperature ($0.5 \times T_{melt}$) is a bulk diffusion proxy; for CO₂ capture, surface diffusion and grain boundary migration are the dominant sintering mechanisms. Your analysis assumes bulk stability implies surface stability, which is not necessarily true for complex polyanionic frameworks.

**3. Missed Opportunity: The "Carbonate-Oxide Pairing" Logic**
Your stoichiometric parsing is a strong start, but it ignores the possibility of *multi-step* or *intermediate* phase transitions. Many industrial sorbents do not cycle between a pure oxide and a pure carbonate; they form intermediate oxycarbonates. By forcing a 1:1 stoichiometric match, you may have excluded the most kinetically viable pathways. Future iterations should allow for reactions where $M_xO_y + CO_2 \rightarrow M_xO_y(CO_3)_z$ (partial carbonation), which often exhibit lower volume expansion and faster kinetics.

**4. Actionable Recommendations for Future Iterations:**
*   **Reconcile the Pareto Front:** Do not weight $\Delta G$, $\Delta V$, and $T_{margin}$ equally. Mechanical failure is a binary "go/no-go" constraint in industrial reactors. Filter by $\Delta V$ first, then rank by $\Delta G$.
*   **Validate the "Goldilocks" Zone:** You identified Li-V-P and Na-Ti-P systems as optimal. Perform a sensitivity analysis on the $CO_2$ partial pressure (e.g., 0.05 bar to 0.5 bar) to see if these "Goldilocks" candidates remain stable across typical flue gas fluctuations.
*   **Refine the Sintering Proxy:** Instead of just $T_{melt}$, calculate the *cohesive energy per atom* as a more direct proxy for surface energy and sintering resistance.
*   **Address the "Missing" Data:** You flagged FeO, CuO, and ZnO as high-value targets. Since these are absent from the stable dataset, perform a "stability prediction" using a simple ML classifier (Random Forest) on the full 40k dataset to see if these compositions are *predicted* to be stable even if not currently in the MP database. This moves the project from "screening" to "discovery."
