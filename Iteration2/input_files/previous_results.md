## Thermodynamic Landscape of Carbonation Reactions

The foundation of this study relies on the rigorous stoichiometric parsing and thermodynamic mapping of 40,923 inorganic materials from the Materials Project database. By isolating pairs of oxide precursors and carbonate products that share identical metal-to-oxygen ratios, the analysis successfully identified 1,671 valid oxide-carbonate reaction pairs distributed across 119 unique metal compositions. To ensure the thermodynamic values accurately reflect the carbonation process, the raw reaction energies ($Δ E$) were corrected by $-7.23$ eV to account for the standard reference energy of CO₂ formation within the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) framework. Following this correction, the average reaction energy across all valid pairs was found to be $-1.867$ eV/CO₂, indicating a general thermodynamic propensity for spontaneous carbonation among the screened materials.

The role of metal identity in dictating carbonation favorability is profound and clearly delineated by the data. An analysis of the primary metals within the candidate pool reveals a strong dependence of the reaction energy on the elemental family. Alkali and alkaline earth metals exhibit highly exothermic carbonation reactions, indicative of exceptionally strong CO₂ binding affinities. For instance, potassium (K) and barium (Ba) demonstrate median $Δ E$ values of $-5.002$ eV/CO₂ and $-3.686$ eV/CO₂, respectively. Calcium (Ca) and sodium (Na) follow closely with median $Δ E$ values of $-2.845$ eV/CO₂ and $-2.601$ eV/CO₂. While these highly negative values guarantee spontaneous CO₂ uptake, they simultaneously imply that the resulting carbonates are excessively stable, thereby requiring prohibitively high energy inputs for regeneration.

Conversely, transition metals and certain post-transition metals present a much more balanced thermodynamic profile. Metals such as manganese (Mn), cobalt (Co), iron (Fe), and copper (Cu) exhibit median $Δ E$ values of $-1.148$, $-1.294$, $-1.389$, and $-1.587$ eV/CO₂, respectively. These values situate these transition metal-based sorbents much closer to, or directly within, the defined "optimal-delta" thermodynamic window of $-1.5$ to $-0.5$ eV/CO₂. This window represents the energetic sweet spot where the carbonation reaction remains thermodynamically spontaneous but is sufficiently reversible to allow for energy-efficient regeneration. The distribution of these reaction energies is visually captured in the first panel of the generated thermodynamic landscape figure, which illustrates the broad variance in $Δ E$ across different primary metals and underscores the transition metals as prime candidates for reversible capture.

In addition to the reaction energy, the experimental accessibility of these materials is governed by their thermodynamic stability, quantified here as the "stability penalty." The stability penalty is defined as the sum of the `energy_above_hull` for both the oxide and carbonate phases in a given reaction pair. A lower penalty indicates that the phases are closer to the convex hull and thus more likely to be synthesizable and stable under operating conditions. The dataset was strictly filtered to include only materials with an individual energy above the hull of ≤ 0.1 eV/atom. Consequently, the average stability penalty across the 1,671 valid pairs is remarkably low at $0.093$ eV/atom, with a maximum observed penalty of $0.2$ eV/atom. The scatter plot of $Δ E$ versus the stability penalty (second panel of the thermodynamic landscape figure) demonstrates that many of the most thermodynamically optimal candidates (those with $Δ E$ between $-1.5$ and $-0.5$ eV/CO₂) also possess near-zero stability penalties. This critical finding confirms that tuning the reaction energy does not inherently require a compromise in material stability, and that the identified optimal candidates are highly likely to be experimentally accessible and robust over multiple capture-regeneration cycles.

## Entropy and Regeneration Temperature ($T_{reg}$) Estimation

While the reaction energy ($Δ E$) provides a proxy for the reaction enthalpy ($Δ H$), the feasibility of a temperature-swing carbon capture process is ultimately dictated by the regeneration temperature ($T_{reg}$). To estimate $T_{reg}$, the solid-phase entropy changes were approximated using the Neumann-Kopp additive rule, which derives the total entropy of a compound from the sum of the entropies of its constituent elements. Factoring in the standard entropy of gaseous CO₂ ($S_{CO_2(gas)} ≈ 213.8$ J/mol·K), the average total entropy change ($Δ S_{total}$) for the carbonation reactions was calculated to be $-187.58$ J/mol·K. This consistently negative entropy change is physically consistent with the transition of CO₂ from a high-entropy gaseous state to a highly ordered solid carbonate phase.

Because the carbonation reaction is both exothermic ($Δ H < 0$) and entropically unfavorable ($Δ S < 0$), the process is spontaneous at low temperatures and reversible at high temperatures. The regeneration temperature, defined as the temperature at which the free energy of the reaction becomes zero ($T_{reg} = Δ H / Δ S$), was calculated for all candidates exhibiting a negative $Δ S_{total}$. The estimated $T_{reg}$ values span a wide range, from $377.1$ K to $2774.8$ K, with an overall average of $965.1$ K.

For industrial applicability, particularly in processes aiming to utilize low-grade waste heat, a target $T_{reg}$ window of 300–600°C ($573.15$–$873.15$ K) is highly desirable. Remarkably, the analysis reveals that 743 candidates—representing 48.4% of the valid reaction pairs—fall squarely within this optimal temperature window. The distribution of these regeneration temperatures is depicted in the third panel of the thermodynamic landscape figure, where a significant density of materials aligns with the highlighted target zone. This substantial pool of candidates demonstrates that by moving away from traditional, highly exothermic sorbents like CaO (which typically requires regeneration temperatures in excess of 900°C), it is entirely feasible to design solid sorbents that can be regenerated using significantly less energy, thereby drastically reducing the parasitic energy load of the carbon capture process.

## Design Space Regression and Substitution Analysis

To systematically navigate the vast chemical space and understand the fundamental atomic drivers of the carbonation reaction energy, a multivariate ordinary least squares (OLS) regression was performed. The model correlated the corrected $Δ E$ values with three primary elemental descriptors of the primary metal: electronegativity, ionic radius, and oxidation state. The regression model achieved an R-squared value of $0.5613$ (Adjusted R-squared = $0.5605$), indicating a moderate but highly statistically significant correlation between these fundamental properties and the macroscopic thermodynamic behavior.

The regression coefficients provide critical insights into the physical chemistry of the M-O and M-CO₃ bonds. Ionic radius emerged as the most dominant factor, exhibiting a strong negative coefficient ($-0.6011$, $p < 1.38 	imes 10^{-134}$). This indicates that larger metal cations strongly correlate with more exothermic (more negative) $Δ E$ values. This behavior is consistent with the stabilization of the large carbonate anion by larger metal cations, a well-known trend in inorganic chemistry where lattice energy matching dictates phase stability. Conversely, electronegativity ($0.1337$, $p = 3.59 	imes 10^{-4}$) and oxidation state ($0.1001$, $p = 0.0053$) displayed positive coefficients. Higher electronegativity implies a more covalent character in the metal-oxygen bond, which tends to destabilize the ionic carbonate phase relative to the oxide, thereby pushing $Δ E$ toward less exothermic (more positive) values. Similarly, higher oxidation states increase the polarizing power of the cation, which can distort and destabilize the carbonate anion, further reducing the exothermicity of the capture reaction. The reliability of these trends is visually supported by the parity plot of predicted versus actual $Δ E$ values, which shows a clear linear alignment along the parity line, despite some expected variance inherent to simplified descriptor models.

Leveraging these established correlations, a virtual doping and substitution screening was conducted to identify "near-miss" candidates that could be tuned into the optimal thermodynamic window. Focusing on magnesium (Mg) as a lightweight, abundant host material (pure Mg predicted $Δ E = -1.302$ eV/CO₂), virtual substitutions were simulated using Vegard’s Law for property interpolation. The screening evaluated transition metal dopants (Ni, Cu, Co, Zn, Fe, Mn) at varying molar fractions ($0.25$, $0.50$, $0.75$), strictly enforcing constraints on oxidation state matching and ionic radius mismatch ($< 20%$ to adhere to the Goldschmidt tolerance factor).

This virtual screening successfully identified 17 hypothetical doped compositions that fall strictly within the optimal $Δ E$ window of $-1.5$ to $-0.5$ eV/CO₂. For example, doping Mg with 75% Nickel ($Mg_{0.25}Ni_{0.75}$) shifts the predicted $Δ E$ to $-1.064$ eV/CO₂, while a 50% Copper substitution ($Mg_{0.5}Cu_{0.5}$) yields a predicted $Δ E$ of $-1.213$ eV/CO₂. These transition metal dopants, possessing higher electronegativities and slightly smaller ionic radii than Mg, effectively weaken the overall carbonate stability just enough to push the thermodynamics into the highly reversible regime. These findings provide concrete, rationally designed targets for experimental synthesis, demonstrating how mixed-metal oxides can be engineered to overcome the thermodynamic limitations of pure binary oxides.

## Feasibility Scoring and Final Candidate Ranking

To synthesize the multifaceted thermodynamic and structural data into actionable guidance for experimentalists, a comprehensive "Feasibility Score" was formulated. This metric quantitatively ranks all valid candidates by evaluating three critical parameters: (1) the proximity of $Δ E$ to the optimal window of $-1.5$ to $-0.5$ eV/CO₂ (weighted at 40%), (2) the minimization of the stability penalty to ensure experimental synthesizability (weighted at 30%), and (3) the proximity of the estimated $T_{reg}$ to the industrial target window of 300–600°C (weighted at 30%).

The application of this scoring system yielded a highly prioritized list of "Design-Ready" materials. The top-ranked candidate, the $ZnCO_3 / ZnO$ pair (primary metal: Zn), achieved a near-perfect Feasibility Score of $0.9909$. This system exhibits a $Δ E$ of $-1.588$ eV/CO₂, a stability penalty of exactly $0.000$ eV/atom (indicating both phases reside perfectly on the convex hull), and an estimated $T_{reg}$ of $817.0$ K ($543.8$°C), placing it perfectly within the low-grade waste heat utilization window.

Following closely are several mixed-metal lithium-transition metal systems. For instance, the $LiMn(CO_3)_2 / LiMnO_2$ system occupies multiple top-tier rankings (Scores ranging from $0.9834$ to $0.9592$) due to its highly favorable $Δ E$ ($≈ -1.66$ eV/CO₂), negligible stability penalties (≤ 0.022 eV/atom), and ideal regeneration temperatures ($≈ 800$–$870$ K). Other notable top-20 candidates include $LiCr(CO_3)_2 / LiCrO_2$ (Score: $0.9777$, $T_{reg}$: $878.9$ K), $LiFe(CO_3)_2 / LiFeO_2$ (Score: $0.9715$, $T_{reg}$: $896.2$ K), and $LiCo(CO_3)_2 / LiCoO_2$ (Score: $0.9699$, $T_{reg}$: $719.8$ K).

The dominance of transition metals and mixed-metal compositions (particularly those incorporating Li alongside Mn, Fe, Co, or Cr) in the top rankings is a direct validation of the design principles uncovered in the regression analysis. The presence of the transition metal tempers the excessive exothermicity typically associated with pure alkali or alkaline earth systems, while the mixed-metal stoichiometry maintains structural stability. ightarrow NaTiPCO_7$ (Score: $0.9630$, $T_{reg}$: $572.6$ K), highlights the potential of polyanionic frameworks to provide the necessary structural scaffolding for reversible CO₂ insertion.

In conclusion, the rigorous thermochemical screening, combined with empirical entropy estimation and multivariate design space mapping, has successfully distilled a dataset of over 40,000 materials down to a highly curated list of optimal CO₂ sorbents. By prioritizing the chemical potential of the M-O bond and strictly enforcing stability constraints, this study provides a reliable, thermodynamically grounded roadmap for the development of next-generation, energy-efficient solid sorbents for industrial carbon capture.
