The previous iterations have focused on bulk thermodynamic properties and stoichiometric pairing, which inherently favor simple, high-symmetry oxides and carbonates. However, the "pulverization" and "sintering" issues identified in previous reports suggest that bulk thermodynamics alone cannot predict cyclic longevity. I hypothesize that the **local coordination environment (LCE)** of the metal cation—specifically the change in the oxygen coordination number (CN) and the polyhedral distortion index during the transition from oxide to carbonate—is a more critical predictor of structural stability and kinetic reversibility than bulk formation energy. 

By calculating the change in coordination environment ($\Delta CN$) and the change in the Bond Valence Sum (BVS) for the metal centers during the carbonation reaction, we can identify "topologically compatible" pairs. These are pairs where the metal-oxygen polyhedra in the oxide precursor are structurally pre-organized to accommodate the carbonate group with minimal lattice strain (low $\Delta$Distortion). I propose to: 
1) Compute the coordination environment for all metals in the 535 valid oxide-carbonate pairs using a Voronoi-based neighbor analysis. 
2) Calculate the "Structural Compatibility Index" (SCI) defined as the inverse of the change in polyhedral distortion and coordination volume between the oxide and carbonate phases. 
3) Rank candidates by a new multi-objective function: $Score = w_1(\Delta G_{700K}) + w_2(SCI)$, where high SCI values prioritize materials that undergo "topotactic-like" transformations, potentially mitigating the mechanical pulverization observed in previous iterations. This will shift the search from purely thermodynamic "optimal-delta" materials to "mechanically-resilient" sorbents.
