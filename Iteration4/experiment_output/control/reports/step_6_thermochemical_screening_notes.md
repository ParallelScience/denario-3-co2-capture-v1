### 1. Stoichiometric Pairing and Compositional Space Mapping

The foundational phase of this thermochemical screening protocol involved the systematic parsing and curation of the initial Materials Project dataset, which comprised 40,923 inorganic, oxygen-containing compounds. To ensure that the subsequent thermodynamic analyses were grounded in physical reality and experimental accessibility, a strict thermodynamic stability threshold was applied. Only materials residing within 0.1 eV/atom of the compositional convex hull were retained. This 0.1 eV/atom threshold is widely recognized in materials informatics as the upper limit for synthesizability, encompassing stable ground-state phases as well as metastable polymorphs that can be kinetically trapped at room temperature. This curation process successfully distilled the dataset to 25,836 unique, highly stable chemical systems.

Following stability filtering, the dataset was bifurcated based on carbon content, yielding 24,754 potential oxide precursors (carbon-free) and 1,082 carbonate products. The core innovation of the parsing algorithm was the extraction of the exact metal stoichiometry from the reduced chemical formulas. By isolating the metal sublattice and matching the metal-to-metal ratios exactly, the algorithm identified valid topotactic or near-topotactic reaction pathways of the form <code>M_xO_y + CO_2 
ightarrow M_xCO_3</code>. This rigorous stoichiometric matching yielded a total of 535 valid oxide-carbonate reaction pairs.

The compositional distribution of these 535 pairs across the targeted metal families provides a comprehensive map of the available chemical space for CO₂ capture. Lithium-based systems heavily dominate the paired dataset with 199 distinct reaction pairs, followed by transition metals such as iron (125 pairs), manganese (77 pairs), cobalt (37 pairs), and copper (38 pairs). The high prevalence of these elements is reflective of the Materials Project's extensive cataloging of battery intercalation cathodes and magnetic materials. However, the dataset also successfully captured a robust representation of the alkaline earth metals that form the backbone of traditional industrial CO₂ capture, including calcium (19 pairs), magnesium (19 pairs), barium (17 pairs), and strontium (10 pairs). This diverse stoichiometric pairing ensures that the subsequent thermodynamic screening evaluates both established industrial benchmarks and novel, unexplored regions of the compositional phase space.

### 2. Thermodynamic Reaction Energy Landscape and DFT Corrections

With the stoichiometric pairs established, the 0 K reaction energy (<code>ΔE</code>) for the carbonation reaction was calculated for all 535 candidates. The reaction energy was normalized per mole of CO₂ to provide a standardized metric of thermodynamic driving force. A secondary, highly stringent stability filter was applied at this stage, requiring both the specific oxide precursor and its corresponding carbonate product to simultaneously possess an energy above the hull of <code>≤ 0.1 eV/atom</code>. Remarkably, all 535 identified pairs satisfied this dual-stability criterion, confirming that the reaction pathways evaluated represent transitions between genuinely accessible, low-energy states rather than hypothetical high-energy artifacts.

A critical methodological step in evaluating the Density Functional Theory (DFT) computed energies was the application of a systematic correction factor. Standard Generalized Gradient Approximation (GGA) functionals, such as the PBE functional utilized by the Materials Project, notoriously struggle to accurately model the overbinding of the O₂ molecule and the strong C=O double bonds in gaseous CO₂. To reconcile the DFT-computed solid-state energies with experimental macroscopic thermodynamics, a correction factor of -7.23 eV per CO₂ molecule was applied to the raw reaction energies. This empirical adjustment aligns the computational convex hull with experimental calorimetry data, ensuring that the calculated <code>ΔE</code> values accurately reflect the true chemical potential difference between the M-O and M-CO₃ bonds.

The resulting corrected reaction energy landscape exhibits profound variance, highlighting the extreme sensitivity of carbonation thermodynamics to metal identity and local coordination environment. At one extreme, complex silicates and phosphates demonstrated highly exothermic, deeply negative carbonation energies. For example, the reaction of <code>Na₄Al₃(SiO₄)₃</code> to form <code>Na₈Al₆Si₆CO₂₇</code> yielded a <code>ΔE</code> of -9.13 eV/CO₂, while the carbonation of <code>Ca₅(PO₄)₃</code> yielded -9.10 eV/CO₂. While these highly negative values indicate a massive thermodynamic driving force for spontaneous CO₂ uptake, they represent a "thermodynamic trap." The energy penalty required to break these highly stable carbonate bonds during the regeneration phase would be prohibitively high, rendering them entirely unsuitable for cyclic industrial applications.

Conversely, aluminum-dominated and scandium-alloyed systems, such as the carbonation of <code>Al₂O₃</code> to <code>Al₂CO</code> (<code>ΔE = 15.38 eV/CO₂</code>), exhibited highly endothermic, positive reaction energies. These materials are chemically inert to CO₂ under standard conditions and possess no spontaneous capture capacity. The identification of these thermodynamic extremes validates the screening methodology and underscores the necessity of targeting the "optimal-delta" region—materials with moderate, slightly negative <code>ΔE</code> values that provide sufficient driving force for capture while minimizing the thermal energy required for subsequent regeneration.

### 3. Gibbs Free Energy, Entropy Approximations, and Thermal Swing Windows

To translate the 0 K DFT reaction energies into actionable engineering metrics, the Gibbs Free Energy of reaction (<code>ΔG(T, P) = ΔH - TΔS</code>) was computed across a temperature range of 500 K to 1200 K. In solid-gas reactions, the entropy change of the solid phases (from oxide to carbonate) is generally an order of magnitude smaller than the entropy change of the gas phase. Therefore, the total entropy of reaction is overwhelmingly dominated by the loss of translational and rotational degrees of freedom of the CO₂ molecule upon fixation into the solid lattice.

A standard entropy of <code>ΔS° = -0.214 kJ/mol/K</code> was utilized. To simulate realistic post-combustion capture conditions—such as those found in the flue gas of coal or natural gas power plants—this entropy was adjusted for a CO₂ partial pressure of <code>P_{CO_2} = 0.15 bar</code>. According to Le Chatelier's principle and the ideal gas law, this reduced partial pressure decreases the thermodynamic driving force for capture, resulting in an effective operational entropy change of approximately -0.00238 eV/K.

Using these parameters, the equilibrium temperature (<code>T_{eq}</code>), defined as the temperature at which <code>ΔG = 0</code>, was calculated for each candidate. The <code>T_{eq}</code> defines the critical pivot point of the thermal swing cycle: the material acts as a spontaneous sorbent below <code>T_{eq}</code> and undergoes spontaneous regeneration (calcination) above <code>T_{eq}</code>.

The thermodynamic behavior of the dataset is comprehensively visualized in the generated multi-panel analysis. Panel (a) illustrates the distribution of <code>ΔE</code> across the primary metal families. The histogram reveals a distinct chemical trend: alkali metals (Li, Na, K) consistently exhibit deeply exothermic reaction energies, forming highly stable carbonates, whereas transition metals (Fe, Mn, Co) cluster closer to zero or positive <code>ΔE</code> values. Panel (b) plots <code>T_{eq}</code> against <code>ΔE</code>, demonstrating the strict linear correlation dictated by the constant entropy approximation. The plot highlights the target operational window of 500 K to 1200 K, effectively isolating materials that can be regenerated using low-to-medium grade industrial waste heat.

Panel (c) provides a critical snapshot of the Gibbs Free Energy evaluated at a representative intermediate temperature of 700 K. At this temperature, the alkaline earth metals (Ca, Mg, Sr, Ba) exhibit <code>ΔG</code> values tightly clustered around zero. This thermodynamic "sweet spot" perfectly explains their historical dominance as reversible CO₂ sorbents; they are uniquely positioned to toggle between capture and release with minimal temperature swings. In contrast, the transition metals largely exhibit positive <code>ΔG</code> at 700 K, indicating non-spontaneous capture, while the alkali metals remain deeply negative, indicating that 700 K is insufficient for their regeneration.

### 4. Sintering Resistance, Impurity Screening, and Electronic Constraints

Beyond thermodynamic reversibility, the industrial viability of a solid sorbent is dictated by its physical and chemical resilience over thousands of thermal cycles. The primary mechanism of capacity fade in high-temperature sorbents (such as in Calcium Looping) is thermal sintering, where the porous structure of the oxide collapses, drastically reducing the active surface area available for gas-solid interaction. Sintering typically initiates at the Tammann temperature, which is empirically approximated as half of the material's absolute melting point.

To quantify thermal robustness, a melting point proxy (<code>T_{melt_proxy}</code>) was derived. The proxy is based on the physical principle that lattice cohesive energy—and thus the melting temperature—scales with the volumetric bond density. The proxy was calculated as the product of the absolute formation energy per atom and the crystallographic density, multiplied by an empirical scaling constant. This constant was rigorously calibrated using well-characterized binary oxides within the dataset. For <code>CaO</code> (known <code>T_{melt} = 3193 K</code>), the calculated scaling constant was 288.04. For <code>MgO</code> (known <code>T_{melt} = 3125 K</code>), the constant was 283.43. The remarkable agreement between these two independent reference points validated the proxy model, and an average scaling constant of 285.73 was applied across the dataset.

A minimum thermal stability threshold of <code>T_{melt_proxy} ≥ 800 K</code> was enforced, successfully filtering out 42 structurally fragile candidates. The remaining 493 materials exhibited a robust median predicted melting point of 2216 K, indicating high resistance to thermal degradation.

Subsequent chemical stability screening addressed the critical issue of flue gas impurities, specifically sulfur oxides (SOx). Materials containing high concentrations of heavy alkali metals (Na, K, Rb, Cs) are highly susceptible to sulfur poisoning. These elements react with SO₂ and O₂ to form highly stable sulfates (e.g., <code>Na₂SO₄</code>) that possess low melting points and cannot be thermally regenerated under standard calcination conditions, leading to rapid and irreversible sorbent deactivation. Filtering out these alkali-containing compounds removed 93 vulnerable candidates, leaving a chemically robust subset of 400 materials.

Finally, the electronic properties of the oxide precursors were evaluated. Efficient thermal regeneration requires the sorbent bed to be uniformly and rapidly heated. Metallic conductors can suffer from parasitic heat losses, undesirable redox side-reactions, and electronic conductivity that complicates microwave or induction heating strategies. Therefore, candidates were filtered to retain only insulators and wide-band-gap semiconductors, defined by a DFT-computed band gap <code>> 0.5 eV</code> and an <code>is_metal</code> flag of False. This electronic filtering refined the pool to 263 highly qualified, thermally stable, and chemically inert candidates.

### 5. Multi-Objective Ranking and Industrial Viability of Optimal-Delta Candidates

To systematically identify the most promising materials for experimental synthesis and validation, the final 263 candidates were ranked using a multi-objective composite scoring function. This function equally weighted three normalized parameters to balance competing material requirements:
1. **Thermodynamic Reversibility (<code>Score_{ΔG}</code>):** Inversely proportional to the absolute value of <code>ΔG</code> at 700 K. Materials with <code>ΔG ≈ 0</code> receive the highest score, representing the "optimal-delta" balance between capture driving force and regeneration energy penalty.
2. **Thermal Robustness (<code>Score_{T_{melt}}</code>):** Directly proportional to the <code>T_{melt_proxy}</code>, favoring materials with high lattice energies that are highly resistant to sintering and pore collapse.
3. **Electronic Insulation (<code>Score_{bg}</code>):** Directly proportional to the band gap, favoring wide-band-gap insulators that ensure stable thermal management.

The resulting top 20 ranked candidates present a compelling synthesis of established industrial benchmarks and novel, high-performance mixed-metal oxides. The highest-ranking candidate identified by the pipeline is the lithium-lanthanum oxide system (<code>LiLaO₂ → LiLa(CO₃)₂</code>), achieving an exceptional composite score of 0.8109. This material exhibits outstanding predicted thermal stability (<code>T_{melt_proxy} ≈ 4385 K</code>), a wide band gap (3.72 eV), and a highly favorable <code>ΔG</code> at 700 K (-1.012 eV). The incorporation of lanthanum serves a dual purpose: its large ionic radius and high charge density significantly stabilize the crystal lattice against thermal sintering, while simultaneously modulating the basicity of the lithium-oxygen bonds to push the carbonation thermodynamics into the optimal thermal swing window.

Crucially, the validity and predictive power of the screening methodology are strongly corroborated by the prominent ranking of traditional alkaline earth oxides. Magnesium oxide (<code>MgO</code>) appears in the 2nd, 4th, and 6th positions (corresponding to different carbonate polymorphs and stoichiometries, such as <code>Mg₃C₂O₇</code> and <code>MgCO₃</code>). <code>MgO</code> boasts <code>ΔG</code> values remarkably close to zero at 700 K (-0.1457 eV and -0.5265 eV) and robust thermal stability (~3150 K). Calcium oxide (<code>CaO</code>), the undisputed workhorse of industrial calcium looping technologies, ranks 13th and 14th, confirming its excellent balance of thermodynamic favorability (<code>ΔG_{700K} = -1.2187 eV</code>) and thermal resilience (<code>T_{melt_proxy} ≈ 3167 K</code>).

Beyond these binary oxides, the ranking highlights the immense, largely untapped potential of ternary and mixed-alkaline-earth oxides. Strontium calcium oxide (<code>SrCaO₂ → SrCa(CO₃)₂</code>, Rank 9), barium magnesium oxide (<code>BaMgO₂ → BaMg(CO₃)₂</code>, Rank 10), and barium calcium oxide (<code>BaCaO₂ → BaCa(CO₃)₂</code>, Rank 11) all demonstrate superior composite scores. These mixed oxides offer a powerful mechanism to break the scaling relations that typically bind capture capacity and regeneration energy. By alloying a highly reactive, strongly basic metal (e.g., Ba or Sr) with a more moderate metal (e.g., Ca or Mg), the overall basicity of the oxygen active sites is finely tuned. This allows the resulting <code>ΔG</code> to be engineered to sit precisely within the optimal operational window, potentially lowering regeneration temperatures and energy penalties without sacrificing capture kinetics.

While the thermodynamic and structural metrics of these top candidates are exceptional, their ultimate industrial viability for gigaton-scale deployment must also consider elemental abundance and cost. For instance, while <code>LiLaO₂</code> and <code>Li₃SiBiO₅</code> (Rank 16) exhibit near-perfect theoretical performance, the high cost and supply chain constraints associated with lanthanum, bismuth, and lithium may restrict their use to specialized, high-value applications (e.g., direct air capture in confined environments or aerospace life support). Conversely, the highly ranked mixed-alkaline-earth oxides (<code>SrCaO₂</code>, <code>BaMgO₂</code>, <code>BaCaO₂</code>) utilize relatively earth-abundant, inexpensive elements, making them highly attractive targets for immediate experimental scale-up in point-source emission capture.

In conclusion, the rigorous thermochemical parsing, thermodynamic correction, and multi-objective filtering of the Materials Project database successfully distilled over 40,000 compounds into a highly actionable list of 263 viable CO₂ sorbents. By prioritizing stoichiometric exactness, thermodynamic reversibility, and physical robustness, this screening not only reaffirms the fundamental efficacy of alkaline earth oxides but also illuminates a promising new frontier of mixed-metal oxides engineered for optimal carbon capture and release.
