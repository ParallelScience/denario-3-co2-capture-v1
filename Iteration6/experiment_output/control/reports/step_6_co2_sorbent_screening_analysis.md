### 1. Stoichiometric Parsing and Reaction Mapping
The initial phase of the investigation focused on systematically identifying viable carbonation reaction pairs from the Materials Project dataset, which comprises 40,923 inorganic compounds. To ensure that the screened materials are experimentally accessible and not merely hypothetical high-energy phases, a strict thermodynamic stability threshold was applied, retaining only entries with an energy above the convex hull (`energy_above_hull`) of $\le 0.1$ eV/atom. This threshold is widely accepted in computational materials science as the upper limit for synthesizability, allowing for the inclusion of metastable phases that might be stabilized at operating temperatures or through specific synthesis routes.

Following this filtration, the chemical formulas of the remaining oxides and carbonates were parsed to extract their precise metal-to-oxygen stoichiometries. By normalizing the compositions to a fixed number of metal atoms, oxide precursors ($M_xO_y$) were successfully mapped to their corresponding carbonate products ($M_xO_z(CO_3)_w$) that share identical metal-to-non-carbonate-oxygen ratios. This rigorous stoichiometric matching yielded a comprehensive set of reaction pairs across various capture element families, effectively isolating the carbonation event from other confounding compositional changes.

The distribution of these matched pairs is highly heterogeneous across the investigated capture elements, as illustrated in the generated bar chart (`data/step_1_matched_pairs_per_element_1_1775791219.png`). Lithium (Li) overwhelmingly dominates the dataset with 565 distinct matched pairs, reflecting the extensive exploration of Li-based compounds in materials databases due to their relevance in battery technologies and solid-state ionics. Iron (Fe) and Manganese (Mn) follow with 200 and 185 pairs, respectively. Alkali and alkaline earth metals traditionally associated with CO₂ capture, such as Sodium (Na, 85 pairs), Potassium (K, 64 pairs), Barium (Ba, 60 pairs), and Calcium (Ca, 22 pairs), also present a substantial number of candidates. Conversely, elements like Magnesium (Mg, 10 pairs), Zinc (Zn, 3 pairs), and Aluminum (Al, 2 pairs) yielded significantly fewer matched pairs within the $\le 0.1$ eV/atom stability window. This distribution highlights the vast, yet unevenly distributed, chemical space available for designing novel CO₂ sorbents beyond conventional binary oxides, pointing toward a rich landscape of multinary compounds.

### 2. Thermodynamic Landscape of Carbonation
To evaluate the thermodynamic driving force of the carbonation reactions, the reaction enthalpy ($\Delta H$) and Gibbs free energy ($\Delta G$) were calculated for each matched pair. The baseline DFT formation energies were utilized to compute $\Delta H$ at 0 K. To accurately model operating conditions, $\Delta G$ was evaluated at a typical flue gas temperature of 700 K and a CO₂ partial pressure of 0.15 bar. The solid-phase vibrational entropy contributions were estimated by calculating the Debye temperature ($\Theta_D$) for each material using the Lindemann criterion, derived from the unit cell density, volume, and number of sites.

The calculation of the Debye temperature provides a macroscopic proxy for the stiffness of the crystal lattice. Because the carbonation reaction involves the transition of a gas-phase molecule (with high translational and rotational entropy) into a constrained solid carbonate lattice, the reaction is inherently entropically unfavorable ($\Delta S < 0$). Therefore, the reaction must be strongly enthalpy-driven ($\Delta H \ll 0$) to achieve spontaneity at elevated temperatures.

The initial calculations utilized the standard GGA reference energy for CO₂ (-11.17 eV/formula unit). However, to align the DFT-computed energies with experimental thermochemical benchmarks and correct for known GGA overbinding errors in gas-phase molecules, a systematic energy correction of 7.23 eV was subsequently applied to the reaction energies. Prior to this correction, the relative distributions of $\Delta H$ and $\Delta G$ provided critical insights into the thermodynamic behavior of different elemental families, as depicted in the multi-panel box plots (`data/step_2_thermo_distributions_2_1775791475.png`).

The uncorrected thermodynamic distributions reveal distinct clustering based on the primary capture element. Alkali metals (K, Na) and heavier alkaline earth metals (Ba, Sr) exhibit lower mean $\Delta G$ values (e.g., K: 4.71 eV/CO₂, Ba: 4.54 eV/CO₂), indicating a stronger intrinsic thermodynamic affinity for CO₂ compared to transition metals like Fe (6.60 eV/CO₂), Mn (6.73 eV/CO₂), and Zn (6.80 eV/CO₂). Calcium and Magnesium, the archetypal CO₂ capture elements, display intermediate mean $\Delta G$ values of 5.82 eV/CO₂ and 6.25 eV/CO₂, respectively. The wide variance observed within specific families, particularly Li and Fe, underscores the profound influence of the secondary metal cations and the specific crystal structure on the overall reaction thermodynamics. This variability is the cornerstone of the present screening strategy: by exploring complex multinary oxides, it is possible to tune the carbonation thermodynamics to an optimal "Goldilocks" zone—where the reaction is sufficiently exothermic to drive spontaneous capture, yet not so stable that regeneration becomes prohibitively energy-intensive.

### 3. Mechanical Integrity and Stability Filtering
A critical, yet often overlooked, aspect of solid-sorbent design is the mechanical degradation induced by volumetric expansion and contraction during cyclic carbonation and calcination. When a solid sorbent reacts with CO₂, the molar volume typically increases because the carbonate ion ($\text{CO}_3^{2-}$) occupies significantly more space than the oxide ion ($\text{O}^{2-}$). If this expansion exceeds the available pore volume of the macroscopic sorbent particle, it leads to pore plugging, which seals off the unreacted core and drastically reduces the effective capture capacity. Conversely, severe contraction during the regeneration phase can induce micro-fractures, leading to particle pulverization and elutriation in fluidized bed reactors.

To address this, the molar volume change ($\Delta V$) was evaluated for each reaction pair. The effective volume of a captured CO₂ molecule within the solid carbonate lattice was empirically estimated from the dataset to be approximately 36.1 Å³. Using this effective volume, the percentage change in solid volume upon carbonation was calculated. A strict mechanical integrity filter was applied, excluding any candidate where $|\Delta V| > 20\%$. This threshold serves as a binary constraint to mitigate the risks of pore blockage and particle pulverization.

The scatter plot of $\Delta V$ versus $\Delta G$ (`data/step_3_delta_V_vs_delta_G_3_1775791595.png`) visually delineates the surviving candidates from the excluded ones. The survival rates across elemental families reveal significant structural constraints. Elements like Ba, Ca, Ni, Sr, and Al exhibited a 100% survival rate, indicating that their crystal lattices can accommodate the insertion of CO₂ without catastrophic volumetric distortion. Lithium-based compounds also showed exceptional structural resilience, with 561 out of 565 candidates (99.29%) passing the filter. In contrast, Magnesium-based candidates suffered a 50% attrition rate (5 out of 10 surviving), and all Zinc-based candidates were excluded. This mechanical filtering drastically reduces the candidate pool to those that possess both the chemical potential for capture and the structural robustness required for long-term industrial cycling. The fact that complex multinary oxides exhibit $\Delta V$ values well below the 20% threshold suggests that their open framework structures can intercalate CO₂ with minimal lattice strain, a profound advantage over simple oxides like CaO.

### 4. Stability Prediction and Sensitivity Analysis
To extend the utility of this dataset toward the discovery of novel, uncharacterized sorbents, a Random Forest classifier was trained to predict the binary `is_stable` status (i.e., residing exactly on the convex hull, energy_above_hull = 0) based on fundamental compositional and structural features (mean electronegativity, mean atomic radius, density, number of sites, volume, and crystal system).

The model achieved a cross-validated ROC-AUC of 0.5587 ± 0.0810, alongside similarly modest precision and recall metrics. The feature importance analysis (`data/step_4_feature_importance_4_1775791914.png`) indicates that density, mean atomic radius, and unit cell volume are the most heavily weighted predictors. However, the low overall predictive performance is a highly informative negative result. It strongly suggests that thermodynamic stability cannot be reliably captured by simple, aggregated compositional heuristics or basic unit cell dimensions. The convex hull is determined by the relative energies of all competing phases within a chemical space. Therefore, the stability of a specific compound is not an absolute property but a relative one, dependent on the existence of other polymorphs or decomposition products. The formation of stable phases is governed by complex, localized quantum mechanical interactions, specific bonding topologies, and electronic band structures that are not adequately represented by the selected macroscopic features. Consequently, while high-throughput DFT remains a reliable tool for stability assessment, simple machine learning models require more sophisticated, graph-based or local-environment descriptors to accurately navigate the convex hull.

For the candidates that passed the mechanical integrity filter, a sensitivity analysis was conducted to evaluate their thermodynamic robustness under varying industrial conditions. Flue gas compositions are rarely static; therefore, the $\Delta G$ of the top 10 candidates was evaluated across a CO₂ partial pressure range of 0.05 bar to 0.5 bar at 700 K. The resulting line plot (`data/step_4_sensitivity_analysis_4_1775791914.png`) demonstrates the logarithmic dependence of $\Delta G$ on pressure. Crucially, the top candidates maintain a negative $\Delta G$ across the entire pressure range. This indicates that these materials will remain thermodynamically driven to capture CO₂ even during significant fluctuations in the flue gas stream, ensuring reliable performance in dynamic industrial environments.

### 5. Pareto Ranking and Optimal Candidate Selection
The final stage of the screening process involved a multi-objective optimization to identify the most promising sorbent precursors. The ideal CO₂ sorbent must satisfy two competing criteria: (1) an optimal reaction free energy ($\Delta G \approx -0.5$ eV/CO₂) that is sufficiently negative to ensure spontaneous capture but close enough to zero to minimize the energy penalty during thermal regeneration, and (2) a high cohesive energy to resist thermal sintering and maintain surface area over thousands of cycles. In the absence of explicit elemental reference energies, the negative formation energy per atom of the oxide precursor was utilized as a robust proxy for cohesive energy.

A 2D Pareto front analysis was constructed to balance the absolute distance of $\Delta G$ from the -0.5 eV target against the cohesive energy proxy. The resulting Pareto plot (`data/step_5_pareto_front_5_1775792062.png`) maps the landscape of the mechanically viable candidates. The analysis successfully identified 11 Pareto-optimal candidates that define the frontier of this thermochemical space.

The top 20 candidates, ranked by their proximity to the optimal $\Delta G$, are detailed in the final dataset (`data/top_20_ranked_candidates.csv`). The results are highly illuminating. The top-ranked candidate is the transformation of the lithium manganese phosphate $\text{LiMnPO}_5$ to its carbonated form $\text{LiMnPCO}_7$ (mp-757828 $\rightarrow$ mp-768693). This reaction exhibits a near-perfect $\Delta G$ of -0.500 eV/CO₂, a minimal volumetric change of -2.28%, and a strong cohesive energy proxy of 2.29 eV/atom.

Other highly ranked candidates include $\text{Li}_2\text{VO}_3 \rightarrow \text{Li}_2\text{VCO}_5$ ($\Delta G = -0.499$ eV/CO₂, $\Delta V = -7.74\%$), $\text{LiCuO} \rightarrow \text{LiCuCO}_3$ ($\Delta G = -0.496$ eV/CO₂, $\Delta V = -4.84\%$), and $\text{LiVO}_2 \rightarrow \text{LiVCO}_4$ ($\Delta G = -0.504$ eV/CO₂, $\Delta V = -1.44\%$).

A striking trend emerges from this ranked list: the overwhelming prevalence of complex, mixed-metal oxides and polyanionic compounds (such as phosphates and silicates) containing Lithium, Transition Metals (Mn, V, Cu, Fe), and Oxygen. Traditional binary oxides like CaO and MgO, while historically significant, do not appear in the top echelon of this optimized landscape. The incorporation of polyanions (like $\text{PO}_4^{3-}$) and secondary transition metals serves to inductively modulate the basicity of the primary capture sites. The strong covalent P-O bonds within the phosphate group withdraw electron density from the transition metal and the reactive oxygen sites. This reduces the basicity of the oxide, thereby lowering the exothermicity of the carbonation reaction compared to a pure metal oxide. This inductive tuning is precisely what shifts the $\Delta G$ from the overly stable regime into the optimal -0.5 eV/CO₂ window.

Furthermore, these complex crystal structures (predominantly Monoclinic, Orthorhombic, and Triclinic systems) appear to provide a rigid structural scaffold that accommodates the insertion of CO₂ with minimal volumetric disruption. For example, the transition from an Orthorhombic to a Monoclinic crystal system in the $\text{LiMnPO}_5$ reaction indicates a symmetry-lowering distortion that accommodates the carbonate group without requiring a complete reconstruction of the lattice, explaining the remarkably low $\Delta V$ of -2.28%.

### 6. Conclusion and Implications for Industrial CO₂ Capture
By integrating rigorous stoichiometric parsing, temperature-dependent thermodynamic modeling, and strict mechanical integrity constraints, this study has systematically distilled a dataset of over 40,000 materials down to a highly curated list of optimal CO₂ sorbent precursors. The identification of complex multinary systems, particularly lithium-transition metal phosphates and vanadates, as Pareto-optimal candidates represents a significant departure from conventional binary oxide sorbents. These materials offer a compelling combination of ideal reaction thermodynamics for low-energy regeneration, exceptional structural stability to prevent mechanical degradation, and high cohesive energy to resist thermal sintering. These findings provide a clear, data-driven roadmap for the experimental synthesis and kinetic characterization of next-generation solid sorbents for industrial carbon capture, demonstrating the power of high-throughput computational screening in accelerating materials discovery for climate change mitigation.
