The current analysis provides a systematic thermodynamic screening, but it suffers from significant methodological flaws that undermine the validity of the conclusions, particularly regarding the "regeneration temperature" and the "Pareto-optimal" rankings.

**Critical Weaknesses:**
1. **Thermodynamic Inconsistency:** The use of $T_{reg} = \Delta H / \Delta S$ with $\Delta H \approx \Delta E$ is physically problematic. $\Delta E$ (DFT energy) is a 0 K internal energy, not an enthalpy. By ignoring the temperature dependence of the heat capacity ($C_p$) and the enthalpy of the gas phase, the calculated $T_{reg}$ values (e.g., 3000 K+) are physically meaningless for industrial applications (where CaO calcines at ~900°C). The "systematic shift" argument does not fix this; it merely masks the fact that the model is not capturing the chemical potential of CO₂ at relevant pressures and temperatures.
2. **Entropy Approximation:** The Dulong-Petit limit ($3R$ per atom) is a high-temperature limit for solids. Using it at 300–600°C for all phases, while ignoring the vibrational entropy of the carbonate vs. oxide, introduces non-random errors that bias the ranking against materials with different bond stiffnesses.
3. **The "Zero-Strain" Fallacy:** The identification of "zero-strain" frameworks (e.g., Na-Al-Si-O) is likely an artifact of the stoichiometric parsing. If the formula parsing matches a complex framework to a carbonate-bearing phase that is not a true chemical transformation, the $\Delta V$ calculation is meaningless. You are comparing the unit cell volume of two different materials; unless they are topotactic, $\Delta V$ does not represent physical expansion/pulverization.
4. **Missing Kinetic/Chemical Reality:** The analysis ignores the fact that many of the identified "optimal" K-based sorbents are highly hygroscopic or form liquid phases (e.g., molten carbonates) at regeneration temperatures, which would lead to rapid sintering—a failure mode not captured by your structural filter.

**Actionable Feedback for Future Iterations:**
1. **Shift from $T_{reg}$ to $\Delta G$:** Instead of a crude $T_{reg}$ estimate, calculate the Gibbs Free Energy of reaction $\Delta G(T, P) = \Delta H - T\Delta S$ at a fixed industrial condition (e.g., 1 bar CO₂, 700 K). Use the Materials Project's `PhaseDiagram` tools to compute the actual reaction energy at finite temperature rather than relying on 0 K DFT energies.
2. **Validate Topotactic Transitions:** For the "zero-strain" candidates, verify if the transformation is topotactic (i.e., the oxygen framework remains intact). If the space groups are unrelated, the volume change is irrelevant to mechanical stability.
3. **Filter by Melting Point:** Incorporate a melting point proxy (e.g., based on bond valence or simple empirical rules) to exclude materials that would melt or sinter at the required regeneration temperature.
4. **Simplify the Objective Space:** Abandon the 3D Pareto optimization until the thermodynamic basis is corrected. Focus on the "Reaction Window" (the temperature range where $\Delta G = 0$) rather than an arbitrary $T_{reg}$ calculation.
5. **Focus on the "Why":** The current report over-claims the superiority of K-based systems. Future work should investigate if these systems are chemically stable in the presence of flue gas impurities (SOx/NOx), which is the primary reason CaO is used despite its limitations.
