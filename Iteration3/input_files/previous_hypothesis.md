The current screening paradigm focuses on bulk thermodynamic properties and static structural constraints, yet it fails to account for the "chemical environment" of the metal cation within the crystal lattice, which dictates the local coordination geometry and the resulting strain energy during the oxide-to-carbonate phase transition. I hypothesize that the mechanical failure (pulverization) identified in previous iterations is not merely a function of volume expansion, but is specifically driven by the mismatch in the "coordination environment" (coordination number and polyhedral connectivity) between the oxide precursor and the carbonate product. 

By calculating the "Coordination Environment Similarity" (CES) score—defined as the overlap between the local coordination polyhedra of the metal cation in the oxide vs. the carbonate—we can identify materials that undergo "topotactic-like" carbonation. I propose to map the CES score against the existing Pareto-front of $\Delta E$ and $T_{reg}$. My hypothesis is that materials with high CES scores will exhibit lower structural degradation during cycling, even if they possess moderate volume expansion, because the local atomic rearrangement required for carbonation is minimized. This will allow us to filter for "kinetically resilient" sorbents that maintain structural integrity over multiple cycles, providing a new dimension of selection beyond pure thermodynamics.
