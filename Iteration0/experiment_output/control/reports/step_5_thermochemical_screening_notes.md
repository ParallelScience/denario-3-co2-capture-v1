### Thermochemical Screening of Metal-Oxide Carbonation via Stoichiometric Parsing and Stability Constraints

#### Data Overview and Pairing

The foundation of this thermochemical screening relies on a high-fidelity dataset comprising density functional theory (DFT) computed properties for inorganic materials, sourced from the Materials Project. The initial dataset contained 40,923 entries. Rigorous data cleaning procedures confirmed the pristine quality of the dataset, revealing zero duplicate entries and zero missing values across critical columns, resulting in a final working set of 40,923 materials. A compositional breakdown indicates a heavy skew toward precursor materials, with 39,133 oxide entries (materials lacking carbon) compared to 1,790 carbonate entries (materials containing carbon). This imbalance accurately reflects the vast, highly diverse compositional and structural space of metal oxides relative to the more chemically constrained space of carbonate products.

Parsing the reduced chemical formulas to extract normalized metal fractions yielded 9,902 unique metal compositions. To evaluate the thermodynamic feasibility of CO₂ capture, a stoichiometric pairing algorithm was implemented to match oxide precursors ($M_xO_y$) with their corresponding carbonate products ($M_x(CO_3)_z$) based strictly on identical normalized metal compositions. This process successfully identified 480 valid reaction pairs across all available polymorph combinations. 

A breakdown of these 480 pairs by primary metal reveals that "Mixed" metal systems (ternary or quaternary oxides containing more than one metal element) dominate the matched dataset, accounting for 240 pairs. Single-metal systems are led by Barium (60 pairs), Potassium (45 pairs), Iron (35 pairs), Sodium (30 pairs), and Manganese (20 pairs), with smaller contributions from Strontium, Calcium, Magnesium, Lithium, Copper, Zinc, Cobalt, and Nickel. When the pairing logic was restricted to select only the single "best" pair per chemical formula—defined as the polymorph with the lowest energy above the convex hull—the number of valid pairs reduced significantly to 54. This sharp reduction underscores the high degree of polymorphism present in the dataset and highlights the necessity of evaluating the full structural landscape rather than relying solely on ground-state approximations.

Furthermore, an explicit oxygen balance check was conducted to ensure the physical validity of the carbonation reactions. The strict stoichiometric constraint $y = 2z$ (representing reactions of the form $MO + CO_2 \rightarrow MCO_3$) was satisfied by 165 pairs. Conversely, 263 pairs satisfied the $y = z$ constraint (representing reactions such as $M_2O + CO_2 \rightarrow M_2CO_3$). The application of these rigorous stoichiometric boundaries ensures that the subsequent thermodynamic calculations are based on balanced, physically meaningful chemical equations.

#### Reaction Energy Landscape

The core metric for evaluating CO₂ capture favorability is the normalized reaction energy, $\Delta E$, defined as the energy difference between the carbonate product and the oxide precursor, minus the energy of the gaseous CO₂ reactant, normalized per mole of CO₂ absorbed: $\Delta E = (E_{carbonate} - E_{oxide} - zE_{CO_2})/z$. To ensure accurate thermodynamic scaling and reliable estimation of macroscopic properties, the reference energy for CO₂ ($E_{CO_2}$) was corrected to -3.94 eV, aligning with the specific generalized gradient approximation (GGA) functional and mixing scheme corrections applied within this dataset.

The distribution of normalized reaction energies varies profoundly across different metal families, as visualized in the generated plots (<code>step_2_delta_E_boxplot_1_20260409_231844.png</code> and <code>step_2_delta_E_distribution_2_20260409_231844.png</code>). Alkali and alkaline earth metals generally exhibit highly exothermic carbonation reactions, indicative of a massive thermodynamic driving force for CO₂ uptake. In contrast, transition metals display a much broader and often less exothermic thermodynamic profile. 

The heatmap analysis (<code>step_2_delta_E_heatmap_3_20260409_231844.png</code>) further elucidates these trends by mapping the fraction of polymorphs falling into specific energy bins. Elements like Calcium and Strontium show narrow, highly concentrated energy distributions, meaning their carbonation thermodynamics are relatively insensitive to the specific polymorph structure. Conversely, Iron, Manganese, and Mixed-metal systems span a remarkably broad range of reaction energies. This variance is driven by the ability of transition metals to adopt multiple oxidation states and diverse coordination environments, which significantly alters the chemical potential of the M-O bond and, consequently, the stability of the resulting carbonate.

#### Stability-Filtered Candidates

For a solid sorbent to be viable for industrial CO₂ capture, it must not only possess favorable carbonation thermodynamics but also exhibit long-term structural stability to survive thousands of high-temperature regeneration cycles. In this dataset, thermodynamic stability is quantified by the `energy_above_hull` metric, where a value of 0 eV/atom indicates a material is on the convex hull (perfectly stable), and values $>0$ indicate metastability.

A strict stability filter was applied to the 480 valid pairs. Only 17 pairs featured both the oxide precursor and the carbonate product perfectly on the convex hull. Relaxing this threshold to a near-hull criterion—requiring both phases to have an energy above hull $\le$ 0.05 eV/atom—expanded the pool to 177 pairs. This subset represents materials that are highly likely to be synthesizable and resistant to irreversible phase decomposition during thermal cycling.

To identify the most promising sorbents, an "optimal-delta" thermodynamic window was defined between -0.5 and -1.5 eV/CO₂. Reactions less exothermic than -0.5 eV/CO₂ typically lack the thermodynamic driving force required for rapid, spontaneous CO₂ uptake at the low partial pressures characteristic of flue gas or ambient air. Conversely, reactions more exothermic than -1.5 eV/CO₂ form excessively stable carbonates (such as bulk CaCO₃), which require prohibitively high temperatures and massive energy inputs to regenerate.

Strikingly, applying the near-hull stability filter ($\le$ 0.05 eV/atom) to the dataset yielded **zero** candidates within the optimal-delta range. This is a profound finding that highlights a fundamental thermodynamic trade-off in materials design for carbon capture: materials that are highly stable in both their oxide and carbonate forms inherently possess carbonation energies outside the ideal operational window. They are either too weakly binding to capture CO₂ effectively or too strongly binding to be regenerated efficiently.

By removing the stability constraint to explore the broader, metastable chemical space, 72 optimal-delta candidates were identified. To contextualize these candidates for industrial application, a proxy for the regeneration temperature ($T_{reg}$) was calculated using the relation $T_{reg} \approx \Delta E / \Delta S$, assuming a standard entropy of CO₂ gas ($\Delta S \approx 213.8$ J/mol·K). The identified candidates exhibit $T_{reg}$ values ranging from near ambient temperatures up to approximately 400 °C, which is highly desirable for utilizing low-grade industrial waste heat.

Notable candidates from this unfiltered optimal list include:
*   **Manganese Systems:** The reaction MnO $\rightarrow$ Mn(CO₃)₂ exhibits $\Delta E$ values between -0.558 and -0.675 eV/CO₂, corresponding to exceptionally low regeneration temperatures of -21.3 °C to 31.5 °C. While the carbonate phases are slightly metastable (hull $\approx$ 0.074 eV/atom), the near-ambient regeneration profile makes them highly intriguing candidates for Direct Air Capture (DAC) applications where minimizing thermal energy input is paramount.
*   **Potassium Systems:** The K₂O₂ $\rightarrow$ KCO₃ transition shows $\Delta E$ values from -0.829 to -0.941 eV/CO₂ ($T_{reg}$ ranging from 101.0 °C to 151.4 °C). The oxide precursor is perfectly stable on the hull, while the carbonate is marginally metastable.
*   **Iron Systems:** Reactions such as Fe₂O₃ $\rightarrow$ Fe₂C₂O₇ and FeO $\rightarrow$ Fe(CO₃)₂ provide a rich thermodynamic landscape, with $\Delta E$ ranging from -0.840 to -1.211 eV/CO₂ ($T_{reg}$ 105.8 °C to 273.3 °C). The ability of iron to easily transition between oxidation states allows for fine-tuning of the capture thermodynamics.
*   **Mixed Metal Oxides:** Lithium-transition metal oxides, including LiVO₃, LiCoO₂, and LiMnO₂, dominate the lower end of the optimal range ($\Delta E$ -0.852 to -1.490 eV/CO₂, $T_{reg}$ 111.3 °C to 399.4 °C). These ternary systems demonstrate that alloying highly electropositive alkali metals with transition metals can effectively modulate the M-O bond strength, pulling the excessively exothermic lithium carbonation reaction into the optimal thermodynamic window.

#### Structural Correlations

To elucidate the underlying physical and chemical drivers of carbonation favorability, a comprehensive correlation analysis was performed between the normalized reaction energy ($\Delta E$) and various elemental and structural descriptors across 474 clean data entries. The statistical results, visualized in <code>step_4_descriptor_correlations_20260409_232333.png</code>, provide clear heuristics for future materials design.

**Elemental Descriptors:**
*   **Pauling Electronegativity (EN):** A strong positive correlation was observed between the mean electronegativity of the metal composition and $\Delta E$ (Pearson r = 0.7544, p = 2.47e-88; Spearman r = 0.6650). As the metal becomes more electronegative, the reaction energy becomes less negative (less exothermic). This aligns perfectly with chemical intuition: highly electropositive metals (low EN) readily donate electron density to form strongly ionic, highly stable carbonate lattices. More electronegative metals form more covalent, weaker M-O bonds within the carbonate structure, thereby reducing the thermodynamic driving force for CO₂ uptake.
*   **Atomic and Ionic Radii:** Both Atomic Radius (AR) and Ionic Radius (IR) exhibit strong negative correlations with $\Delta E$ (AR Pearson r = -0.6688; IR Pearson r = -0.6898). Larger cations lead to more exothermic carbonation reactions. This is a manifestation of lattice energy dynamics: large, low-charge-density cations are better suited to stabilize the large, polyatomic carbonate anion (CO₃²⁻) compared to small cations, which sterically and electrostatically prefer the smaller oxide anion (O²⁻).

**Macroscopic Structural Proxies:**
In stark contrast to the elemental descriptors, macroscopic structural features of the oxide precursor showed weak or negligible correlations with carbonation thermodynamics. The Oxide Volume ($\text{\AA}^3$) exhibited virtually no correlation (Pearson r = -0.1034), and the Oxide Density (g/cm³) showed only a weak positive correlation (Pearson r = 0.3542).

#### Discussion and Industrial Implications

The findings of this study provide a robust theoretical framework for the design and selection of next-generation solid sorbents for CO₂ capture. The correlation analysis definitively proves that the chemical potential of the M-O bond—dictated fundamentally by the metal's electronegativity and ionic radius—is the primary determinant of carbonation thermodynamics. Macroscopic structural proxies, such as unit cell volume or density, are secondary and lack predictive power for thermodynamic screening.

The identification of 72 optimal-delta candidates with estimated regeneration temperatures between ambient and 400 °C represents a significant pathway toward lowering the parasitic energy penalty of carbon capture. Traditional processes, such as calcium looping (CaO/CaCO₃), require regeneration temperatures upwards of 900 °C, which is highly energy-intensive and leads to rapid sorbent degradation via sintering. Shifting the operational window to 100–400 °C allows for the integration of low-grade industrial waste heat or low-cost renewable thermal energy, drastically improving the techno-economic viability of the capture process.

However, the discovery that zero candidates exist within the optimal thermodynamic window while simultaneously maintaining strict phase stability (energy above hull $\le$ 0.05 eV/atom) highlights a critical engineering challenge. Operating in the optimal thermodynamic regime inherently requires navigating metastable phase spaces. This implies that purely thermodynamic screening is insufficient for deploying these materials at scale. Future sorbent engineering must incorporate kinetic stabilization techniques—such as nanostructuring, surface doping, or encapsulation within inert porous matrices—to prevent the degradation of these thermodynamically ideal, yet slightly metastable, phases over thousands of capture and regeneration cycles.

Ultimately, the established correlations offer a direct recipe for elemental substitution: to move a highly stable but overly exothermic sorbent (e.g., MgO) into the optimal $\Delta E$ window, materials scientists should substitute the primary metal with dopants that possess higher Pauling electronegativity and smaller ionic radii. This theoretical insight directly validates and guides the experimental practice of utilizing mixed-metal and transition-metal-doped oxides to achieve highly efficient, low-temperature CO₂ capture.
