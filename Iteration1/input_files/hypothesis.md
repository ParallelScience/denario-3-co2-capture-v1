The current thermodynamic screening approach is limited by the assumption that bulk-phase stability (convex hull) is the primary determinant of sorbent performance. However, CO₂ capture is a surface-mediated process where the local coordination environment of the metal cation dictates the activation energy of the carbonation reaction. I hypothesize that the "stability-performance trade-off" observed in previous iterations can be bypassed by identifying materials with high "Structural Flexibility" (defined as the ratio of unit cell volume to the number of atoms, normalized by the coordination number of the metal site). By training a Graph Neural Network (GNN) to predict the local site-specific formation energy—rather than bulk formation energy—we can identify metastable oxides that possess "open" structural motifs (e.g., high-symmetry, low-density frameworks) which facilitate CO₂ diffusion and carbonate nucleation. This approach shifts the focus from bulk thermodynamic stability to "kinetic accessibility," allowing us to select candidates that are thermodynamically favorable for carbonation but structurally predisposed to low-energy regeneration, effectively decoupling the stability penalty from the capture capacity.
