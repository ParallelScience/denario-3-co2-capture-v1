The current thermodynamic screening approach is limited by the "stability-performance" trade-off, where optimal carbonation energies are only found in metastable phases. I hypothesize that the "missing" performance in stable materials is due to the neglect of local coordination environments and the electronic influence of the metal-oxygen bond's covalency. Instead of relying on bulk thermodynamic proxies, I propose a "Coordination-Electronic Descriptor" (CED) model. By calculating the average coordination number (CN) of the metal atoms and the band gap (as a proxy for electronic localization) of the oxide precursors, we can identify "hidden" stable sorbents. Specifically, I hypothesize that oxides with high-symmetry coordination environments (e.g., octahedral vs. tetrahedral) and specific band-gap ranges exhibit lower activation barriers for CO₂ insertion, even if their bulk $\Delta E$ is slightly outside the "optimal" range. By training a machine learning model to predict the carbonation reaction energy $\Delta E$ using these structural-electronic descriptors alongside the existing composition features, we can identify stable materials that possess the necessary electronic structure to facilitate CO₂ capture, effectively bypassing the reliance on metastable phases.
